Court System of Praya

Structure, jurisdiction, and administration of the Republic's judicial branch

Judicial Hierarchy

The judicial system of the Republic of Praya is established under Part II of the Criminal Code. All courts are independent in the exercise of their functions and subject only to the Constitution and the laws of Praya.

Supreme Court of Praya
Article 10 — Highest Judicial Authority

The Supreme Court is the highest judicial authority in Praya, presided over by the Chief Justice and six Associate Justices.

Jurisdiction includes:

  • Original jurisdiction in constitutional matters
  • Appellate jurisdiction over Court of Appeal decisions
  • Authority to issue advisory opinions on constitutional interpretation
  • Judicial review of legislative and executive actions
Chief Justice: Hon. Marcus Chen (Since 2019)
Location: Supreme Court Building, Government Complex, 18 Russell St.
Court of Appeal
Article 11 — Appellate Review

The Court of Appeal reviews decisions from District Courts and specialized courts, organized into three divisions.

Jurisdiction includes:

  • Appeals from District Court decisions
  • Appeals from specialized courts (Family, Juvenile)
  • Interlocutory appeals on points of law
Presiding Justice: Hon. Sarah Nakamura
Divisions: Criminal Appeals, Civil Appeals, Administrative Appeals
District Courts
Article 12 — Trial Courts

District Courts have original jurisdiction over criminal and civil matters throughout Praya's four administrative districts.

Districts:

  • Western District — 2 Justices (incl. Government Complex)
  • Eastern District — 2 Justices
  • Northern District — 2 Justices
  • Southern District — 2 Justices

Specialized Courts

National Family Court
Article 13 — Family Matters

Jurisdiction includes:

  • Domestic violence offenses
  • Offenses committed against children
  • Child custody and welfare matters
  • Family-related civil matters
Juvenile Court
Article 14 — Youth Rehabilitation

Operating under the Justice Department, the Juvenile Court has jurisdiction over offenses committed by persons under 18 years of age, with emphasis on rehabilitation and educational measures rather than punitive sanctions.

Office of the Prosecutor-General

Investigation, prosecution, and execution of criminal justice in Praya

Role of the Justice Department

Under Article 15 of the Criminal Code, the Justice Department is responsible for investigating criminal offenses, prosecuting offenders, and executing court judgments. The Department is headed by the Prosecutor-General of Praya.

VO
Dr. Victoria Okonkwo
Prosecutor-General of Praya
In office since March 15, 2023

Dr. Okonkwo brings 18 years of prosecutorial experience to the office, having previously served as Chief Prosecutor of the Eastern District and Deputy Attorney General for Criminal Affairs. She holds a J.D. from Praya National University and a Ph.D. in Criminal Justice from Cambridge University.

Under her leadership, the Department has achieved an 87.2% conviction rate while reducing average case processing time by 15%.

Prosecutorial Functions

Article 16 outlines the core responsibilities of the Prosecutor-General:

  • Direct and supervise all prosecutorial functions throughout Praya
  • Ensure uniform application of criminal law across all districts
  • Issue guidelines on prosecution policy
  • Represent the Republic in criminal proceedings
  • Oversee victim support and witness protection programs

Prosecution Guidelines

All prosecutorial decisions are guided by the following principles established in the Department's Prosecution Manual:

  • Evidential Test: Sufficient evidence must exist to provide a realistic prospect of conviction
  • Public Interest Test: Prosecution must serve the interests of justice and society
  • Proportionality: Charging decisions should reflect the severity of the offense
  • Victim Consideration: Views of victims should be sought and considered
  • Disclosure Obligations: All relevant evidence must be disclosed to the defense

Criminal Code of Praya

Official codification of criminal offenses and penalties

Classification of Offenses (Article 17)

Under the Criminal Code, offenses are classified into three categories based on severity:

Category Definition Imprisonment Range
Felonies Most serious offenses Exceeding 3 years
Misdemeanors Moderately serious offenses 6 months to 3 years
Infractions Minor offenses Less than 6 months or fine

Penalties (Article 18)

Principal Penalties

  • Capital Punishment — Reserved for most serious offenses (Article 19)
  • Imprisonment — Terms ranging from infractions to life (Article 20)
  • Fines — Calculated in Praya Dollar(s) (Article 21)
  • Community Service — Alternative for appropriate cases

Accessory Penalties

  • Disqualification from public office
  • Prohibition from practicing a profession
  • Confiscation of instruments or proceeds of crime
  • Publication of judgment
  • Restitution to victims

Capital Offenses (Article 19)

Capital punishment may only be imposed for: First-degree murder with aggravating circumstances, high treason in time of war, espionage resulting in death or severe national security damage, and large-scale drug trafficking with aggravating circumstances. No person under 18 at the time of offense may receive capital punishment.

Special Note: Griefing (Article 30.3)

Willful destruction of constructed structures or built environments carries imprisonment for 1 to 5 years, plus mandatory restitution and community service to repair damage. This offense is prosecuted aggressively to protect Praya's built heritage.

Rights of the Accused (Article 40)

All accused persons are guaranteed the following rights under the Criminal Code:

  • Presumption of innocence until proven guilty
  • Prompt notification of charges
  • Adequate time and facilities to prepare a defense
  • Trial without undue delay
  • Right to examine witnesses and evidence
  • Right to legal representation

Self-Defense Doctrine (Article 41)

Praya recognizes the right to use reasonable force in self-defense and defense of others. The Castle Doctrine permits use of force, including deadly force, to prevent unlawful entry when the intruder is believed to intend felony or serious harm. No duty to retreat exists within one's dwelling.
