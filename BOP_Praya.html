Monetary Policy

Price Stability & Economic Growth

The Bank of Praya uses interest rate policy, reserve requirements, and open market operations to achieve its dual mandate of stable prices and full employment.

Policy Framework

The Monetary Policy Committee (MPC) targets an annual inflation rate of 2.0% ± 0.5%, measured by the Consumer Price Index. The policy rate is reviewed monthly, with decisions based on employment data, GDP growth, inflation expectations, and external sector balances.

The MPC operates under a flexible inflation targeting regime established by the Central Bank Act of 2008. This framework grants the Bank operational independence while maintaining democratic accountability through regular reporting to Parliament. The Committee comprises seven members: the Governor (chair), two Deputy Governors, and four external experts appointed for staggered five-year terms to ensure continuity and diverse perspectives.

Policy decisions are reached through consensus-building, though dissenting votes are recorded and published. Each meeting begins with staff presentations on economic developments, followed by deliberations on the appropriate policy stance. The Committee considers a wide range of indicators including real-time payment data, business sentiment surveys, global commodity prices, and financial market conditions alongside traditional macroeconomic statistics.

Current Policy Rate: 3.75%

The MPC raised the rate by 25 basis points in January 2025 to moderate demand-driven inflation pressures while supporting sustainable growth. This marks the sixth consecutive increase since mid-2024, reflecting the Committee's determination to anchor inflation expectations within the target band while labor markets remain tight and wage growth accelerates.

Transmission Mechanisms

  • Interest rate channel: Policy rate influences commercial lending rates, affecting consumption and investment.
  • Exchange rate channel: Rate differentials impact the Prayan Crown, influencing net exports.
  • Asset price channel: Monetary conditions affect equity and housing markets, altering household wealth.
  • Credit channel: Bank lending standards tighten or ease in response to policy signals.

Forward Guidance

The MPC expects to maintain a gradual tightening bias through mid-2026, contingent on labor market strength and inflation remaining within the target band. The Committee's baseline scenario assumes continued economic expansion at a moderate pace, with core inflation gradually converging to the 2% midpoint by year-end. However, risks are tilted to the upside given persistent supply constraints in key sectors and elevated wage settlements.

Forward guidance serves as a critical policy tool, shaping market expectations about the future path of interest rates. By communicating the Committee's reaction function and conditional commitments, the Bank influences long-term interest rates, exchange rate dynamics, and investment decisions even without immediate policy changes. During the pandemic recovery, forward guidance proved particularly effective in maintaining accommodative financial conditions while the policy rate remained at the lower bound.

Unconventional Tools

In extraordinary circumstances, the Bank may deploy asset purchase programs, negative interest rates, or targeted lending facilities to stabilize the financial system. Our unconventional toolkit was extensively utilized during the 2020-2022 pandemic crisis, when the MPC implemented a comprehensive package including:

  • Quantitative Easing: Purchase of ¤45 billion in government bonds and investment-grade corporate securities to lower long-term interest rates and support credit supply. These holdings have been fully unwound as of Q4 2024 through gradual portfolio runoff.
  • Targeted Long-Term Refinancing Operations (TLTROs): Three-year loans to banks at favorable rates conditional on maintaining or expanding lending to businesses and households, particularly small and medium enterprises.
  • Asset-Backed Securities Purchases: Acquisition of highly-rated securities backed by auto loans, mortgages, and equipment leases to unfreeze credit markets and reduce borrowing costs for consumers and firms.
  • Foreign Exchange Swap Lines: Coordination with major central banks to provide Praya Dollar(s) liquidity to foreign financial institutions and ensure smooth functioning of international payment systems.

While these measures proved effective in stabilizing markets and supporting economic recovery, the MPC remains mindful of potential side effects including asset price distortions, excessive risk-taking, and reduced market functioning. Future deployments would be calibrated carefully and unwound systematically once financial conditions normalize.

Banking Supervision

Prudential Oversight & Financial Stability

The Bank of Praya licenses, regulates, and supervises commercial banks, credit unions, and payment institutions to ensure system-wide resilience.

Licensing & Authorization

New banking applicants must demonstrate adequate capital, qualified governance, sound business models, and anti-money laundering controls. Minimum paid-in capital for a retail bank is ¤50 million.

  • Retail banking licenses (deposits, loans, payments)
  • Wholesale banking licenses (no retail deposits)
  • Payment institution licenses (e-money, transfers)
  • Credit union charters (member-owned cooperatives)

Prudential Standards

All institutions must maintain a minimum Common Equity Tier 1 (CET1) ratio of 7%, with additional buffers for systemically important banks. Liquidity coverage ratio (LCR) must exceed 100%, ensuring 30-day survival in a stress scenario. Net stable funding ratio (NSFR) requirements ensure institutions maintain stable funding profiles relative to asset composition and off-balance sheet activities.

Our capital framework aligns with international Basel III standards while incorporating macroprudential elements tailored to Praya's financial system. Systemically important domestic banks (D-SIBs) face capital surcharges ranging from 1% to 2.5% based on their systemic footprint, measured by size, interconnectedness, complexity, and substitutability. The countercyclical capital buffer (CCyB), currently set at 0.5%, can be raised to 2.5% during periods of excessive credit growth to build resilience against systemic risks.

Leverage ratio requirements complement risk-based capital standards, setting a minimum 3% ratio of Tier 1 capital to total exposures including off-balance sheet items. This backstop prevents excessive leverage and provides a simple, transparent measure of bank solvency that is less susceptible to risk-weight optimization.

Supervisory Activities

On-site examinations occur annually for large banks, biennially for smaller institutions. Continuous monitoring uses regulatory reports (capital, liquidity, large exposures) and real-time transaction surveillance via RTGS feeds. Our supervisory approach combines:

  • Point-in-time assessments: Detailed evaluations of capital adequacy, asset quality, management capability, earnings, liquidity, and sensitivity to market risk (CAMELS framework).
  • Forward-looking analysis: Reviews of strategic plans, risk appetite frameworks, scenario testing capabilities, and governance structures to identify emerging vulnerabilities.
  • Thematic reviews: Cross-institutional examinations of specific risk areas such as cyber resilience, climate risk management, commercial real estate exposures, or fintech partnerships.
  • Model validation: Independent assessment of internal models used for credit risk, market risk, and operational risk capital calculations under the advanced measurement approaches.

Supervisory intensity is calibrated to institutional risk profiles using a risk-based framework. High-risk institutions receive more frequent on-site visits, deeper examinations, and enhanced reporting requirements. Well-capitalized, well-managed institutions with strong compliance cultures benefit from streamlined supervision while maintaining prudential standards.

Resolution Framework

The Bank maintains resolution plans for all systemically important institutions. In the event of failure, resolution tools include purchase-and-assumption, bridge bank, and bail-in of subordinated creditors. Our resolution regime, established under the Financial Institutions Resolution Act (2019), aims to maintain critical financial services, protect depositors and public funds, and minimize systemic disruption while imposing losses on shareholders and creditors in accordance with the creditor hierarchy.

Resolution planning is a continuous process requiring institutions to maintain adequate loss-absorbing capacity, ensure operational continuity in resolution scenarios, and provide comprehensive information about their legal structures, business lines, and critical dependencies. The Bank conducts annual resolvability assessments, identifying impediments and requiring remedial actions such as corporate simplification, establishment of service level agreements for critical functions, or pre-positioning of collateral.

Our resolution toolkit encompasses several mechanisms:

  • Purchase and Assumption: Sale of viable parts of a failing institution to a healthy acquirer, with the Bank facilitating transactions through loss-sharing arrangements or asset guarantees if necessary.
  • Bridge Bank: Transfer of critical operations to a temporary publicly-owned institution, maintaining service continuity while seeking permanent solutions through eventual sale or wind-down.
  • Bail-In: Conversion of subordinated debt and eligible senior debt into equity to recapitalize the institution, imposing losses on private creditors rather than taxpayers while keeping the bank operational.
  • Asset Separation: Transfer of impaired or non-core assets to a separate vehicle for orderly disposal, de-risking the remaining institution and facilitating recovery or sale.

The Deposit Insurance Fund, capitalized through risk-based premiums on insured deposits, provides up to ¤100,000 coverage per depositor per institution. In resolution scenarios, the Fund may provide liquidity support, purchase assets, or make equity investments to facilitate least-cost resolutions while protecting insured depositors.

Consumer Protection

Retail customers are protected by deposit insurance (up to ¤100,000 per depositor), transparent fee disclosures, and access to the Banking Ombudsman for dispute resolution.

Financial Markets

Market Operations & Currency

The Bank implements monetary policy through open market operations, manages foreign reserves, and issues the national currency.

Open Market Operations

The Bank conducts daily repo and reverse repo operations to steer overnight interbank rates toward the policy target. Primary dealers submit bids via the electronic trading platform, with settlements occurring in real time. These operations represent the primary mechanism through which monetary policy intentions are transmitted to broader financial markets.

Our operational framework distinguishes between regular liquidity-providing operations (repos) and liquidity-absorbing operations (reverse repos). Repo auctions typically occur at 10:00 each business day, with results published within 15 minutes. Eligible collateral includes government securities, highly-rated corporate bonds, and covered bonds meeting strict credit quality criteria. Haircuts are applied based on security type, maturity, and credit rating to mitigate counterparty risk.

Primary dealers—currently numbering 12 institutions—are obligated to participate meaningfully in auctions, maintain bilateral communication with the Markets Division, and provide liquidity in secondary markets for government securities. In return, they receive privileged access to the Bank's operations and regular market intelligence briefings. Dealer performance is monitored continuously, with annual reviews determining retention of primary dealer status.

During periods of market stress, the Bank may conduct supplementary longer-term operations (1-week or 1-month tenors), expand collateral eligibility, or utilize outright purchases of securities to ensure adequate liquidity provision and support smooth market functioning.

Standing Facilities

  • Lending facility: Banks can borrow overnight at policy rate + 50bp against eligible collateral. Available from 16:00 to 17:00, this facility provides a safety valve for institutions facing unexpected liquidity shortfalls, preventing overnight rates from spiking excessively above the target.
  • Deposit facility: Excess reserves earn policy rate - 50bp, creating a symmetric corridor. Banks may deposit unlimited amounts from 16:00 to 17:00, establishing a floor for overnight rates and absorbing surplus liquidity when market conditions are accommodative.

The standing facilities framework creates a ±50 basis point corridor around the policy rate, with overnight market rates typically trading within this band. This corridor system provides certainty to market participants while allowing the Bank to fine-tune liquidity provision through daily operations. During normal times, minimal volumes pass through the facilities, indicating well-calibrated liquidity management. Elevated usage signals market dysfunction requiring investigation and potential intervention.

Foreign Exchange Management

The Bank maintains foreign reserves equivalent to six months of imports, primarily in USD, EUR, and gold. The Praya Dollar(s) (PRD) floats freely, with interventions limited to smoothing excessive volatility. Our reserves management framework balances three objectives: maintaining adequate liquidity for potential interventions, preserving capital value, and generating reasonable returns within strict risk parameters.

The reserve portfolio is diversified across multiple currencies (60% USD, 25% EUR, 10% other major currencies, 5% gold) and instrument types (short-term deposits, government bonds, supranational securities, and gold bullion). Portfolio composition is reviewed quarterly by the Reserve Management Committee, which may adjust allocations based on market developments, currency trends, and evolving reserve adequacy needs.

Exchange rate policy follows a floating regime with managed flexibility. The Bank does not target specific exchange rate levels but may intervene to counter disorderly market conditions characterized by excessive volatility, herding behavior, or temporary liquidity gaps. Intervention decisions are discretionary and non-automatic, announced publicly within two business days to maintain transparency while preserving operational flexibility.

Recent interventions have been infrequent—totaling three operations in 2024—and limited in scale, reflecting well-functioning foreign exchange markets and the credibility of our policy framework. The Praya Dollar(s) has appreciated modestly against major currencies over the past year, supported by strong export performance, robust foreign direct investment inflows, and favorable interest rate differentials.

Securities Markets

The Bank works closely with the Praya Stock Exchange (PSE) to ensure efficient capital markets and support the transmission of monetary policy through asset prices. The PSE provides a transparent marketplace for trading equities, bonds, and derivatives, contributing to financial market depth and corporate financing options. Regular coordination between the Bank and PSE ensures alignment on market infrastructure standards, settlement procedures, and crisis management protocols.

Currency Management

The Bank issues and distributes banknotes in denominations of 5, 10, 20, 50, 100, and 200 Praya Dollar(s)(s). Polymer notes feature advanced security threads, color-shifting ink, and tactile features for visually impaired users. Each denomination showcases a different theme celebrating Prayan heritage: the ¤5 note depicts marine biodiversity, ¤10 features agricultural traditions, ¤20 highlights scientific achievements, ¤50 commemorates the independence movement, ¤100 showcases architectural landmarks, and ¤200 honors cultural festivals.

Currency in circulation totals approximately ¤22 billion, growing at 4-5% annually in line with economic expansion and inflation. Despite rapid digitalization of payments, cash remains important for small-value transactions, rural commerce, and as a store of value during emergencies. The Bank maintains strategic cash reserves at six regional distribution centers, ensuring continuity of supply during natural disasters or infrastructure disruptions.

Counterfeit detection is supported by public education campaigns, regular training sessions for bank staff and retailers, and provision of authentication devices to high-volume cash handlers. Counterfeit rates remain exceptionally low at fewer than 10 parts per million, reflecting sophisticated security features and effective law enforcement cooperation. Detected counterfeits are confiscated, analyzed by forensic specialists, and intelligence is shared with international partners through Interpol channels.

Environmental sustainability guides our currency lifecycle management. Polymer notes last 2.5 times longer than paper equivalents, reducing replacement frequency and environmental impact. Worn notes are shredded and recycled into construction materials or composted, achieving near-zero waste. The transition to polymer, completed in 2022, has reduced the carbon footprint of currency production by 32% while improving durability and hygiene.

Digital Currency

A central bank digital currency (CBDC) pilot launched in 2024, allowing select merchants and consumers to hold digital Praya Dollar(s) in regulated wallets. Full rollout is expected by 2026. The Praya Digital Dollar(s) (PRD-e) pilot currently involves 5,000 consumers, 250 merchants, and 8 commercial banks across three cities, testing core functionality including peer-to-peer transfers, merchant payments, and interoperability with existing payment systems.

Our CBDC architecture features a two-tier model: the Bank issues digital currency and maintains the core ledger, while commercial banks and licensed payment service providers distribute wallets and handle customer relationships. This design leverages private sector innovation in user interfaces and customer service while preserving central bank control over monetary policy and financial stability.

Privacy protections balance legitimate user expectations with anti-money laundering requirements. Small-value transactions are pseudonymous, with the Bank unable to trace individual payments without a court order. Larger transactions trigger enhanced due diligence and reporting obligations similar to existing banking regulations. Technical infrastructure uses distributed ledger technology adapted for central bank requirements: high throughput (10,000 transactions per second), instant finality, and operational resilience through redundant nodes.

Policy objectives for CBDC deployment include financial inclusion (providing low-cost digital payment access to unbanked populations), payment system efficiency (reducing reliance on expensive card networks), and monetary policy effectiveness (enabling precise implementation including potential negative interest rates if needed). Extensive stakeholder consultations continue regarding interest-bearing versus non-interest-bearing CBDC, holding limits to mitigate bank disintermediation risks, and offline payment capabilities for emergency scenarios.

Research & Data

Economic Analysis & Statistics

The Bank's research division publishes forecasts, policy analysis, and macroeconomic data to inform policymakers, academics, and the public.

Publications

  • Monetary Policy Report: Quarterly assessment of economic conditions, inflation outlook, and policy stance. Each report contains detailed analysis of recent macroeconomic developments, sectoral trends, external sector performance, and financial market conditions. The centerpiece is the staff economic projection, presenting baseline forecasts for GDP growth, inflation, and unemployment over a two-year horizon, accompanied by alternative scenarios and confidence intervals. The report concludes with an explanation of the MPC's policy decision and forward guidance. Published in February, May, August, and November.
  • Financial Stability Report: Semi-annual analysis of systemic risks, banking sector health, and macro-prudential measures. This comprehensive assessment evaluates vulnerabilities in the financial system through stress testing results, analyses of credit growth patterns, real estate market dynamics, household and corporate debt levels, and cross-border exposures. The report includes the Bank's assessment of systemic risk intensity and announces any changes to macro-prudential policy settings including the countercyclical capital buffer. Published in June and December.
  • Economic Bulletin: Monthly summary of key indicators, labor market trends, and external sector balances. This concise publication provides timely updates on inflation, employment, industrial production, retail sales, trade balances, and monetary aggregates. Special feature articles analyze emerging economic issues such as productivity trends, demographic shifts, technological disruption, or regional economic disparities. The Bulletin serves as a key communication tool for keeping markets and the public informed between quarterly policy reports.
  • Working Papers: Academic research on monetary transmission, banking regulation, and payment system innovation. Our research staff and visiting scholars publish rigorous, peer-reviewed papers advancing theoretical and empirical knowledge in central banking. Recent topics include the effectiveness of forward guidance in a small open economy, optimal design of macro-prudential policies, machine learning applications in inflation forecasting, the impact of climate change on financial stability, and the economics of central bank digital currencies. All working papers are published online with open access.

In addition to these flagship publications, the Bank produces specialized reports on topics such as payment system oversight, consumer credit trends, housing finance, external debt sustainability, and central bank governance. Our publication schedule is announced annually, with release dates adhered to strictly to ensure predictability for market participants and researchers.

Statistical Releases

The Bank publishes real-time data on interest rates, exchange rates, monetary aggregates (M1, M2, M3), credit growth, and payment system volumes. Historical series are available via the statistics portal in CSV and JSON formats. Our statistical program adheres to international standards for quality, transparency, and accessibility, meeting IMF Special Data Dissemination Standards (SDDS) requirements.

Daily releases include overnight interbank rates, standing facility usage, foreign exchange reference rates, and payment system transaction volumes. Weekly publications cover commercial bank reserve positions, central bank balance sheet developments, and government securities auction results. Monthly datasets provide detailed breakdowns of monetary aggregates, credit by sector and maturity, deposit interest rates, lending rates by category, and external sector statistics including trade balances, capital flows, and reserve asset composition.

All statistical releases follow a pre-announced schedule with exact release times (typically 09:00 or 16:00) to ensure fair access. Methodology documents explain data definitions, collection procedures, compilation methods, and any revisions to historical series. Microdata from our surveys can be accessed by approved researchers through secure facilities, subject to confidentiality protections and ethics review.

Our data portal features interactive visualization tools enabling users to create custom charts, download data in multiple formats, and access API endpoints for automated retrieval. Time series span from 2008 (the Bank's establishment) to present, with some series extending further back through historical reconstructions. Mobile-optimized interfaces ensure accessibility across devices.

Economic Forecasts

Quarterly GDP forecasts are produced using a dynamic stochastic general equilibrium (DSGE) model calibrated to Praya's economy. Forecasts include confidence intervals and scenario analysis. Our modeling framework, known as PRAYA-MOD, incorporates sophisticated features including sticky prices and wages, investment adjustment costs, external sector dynamics with terms of trade shocks, financial frictions affecting credit supply, and expectations formation combining forward-looking and adaptive elements.

Model parameters are estimated using Bayesian techniques applied to Prayan macroeconomic data since 1990, supplemented by calibration based on microeconomic evidence and international comparisons. Regular re-estimation ensures the model adapts to structural changes in the economy such as evolving trade patterns, financial deepening, or productivity shifts. Alternative models including semi-structural approaches and machine learning methods provide robustness checks and early warning signals.

Forecast accuracy is evaluated continuously through retrospective analysis comparing projections to actual outcomes. Over the past five years, our one-year-ahead GDP growth forecasts have achieved a mean absolute error of 0.6 percentage points, while inflation forecasts averaged 0.4 percentage points deviation. These metrics compare favorably with international peer central banks and commercial forecasters.

Research Collaboration

The Bank hosts academic conferences, offers visiting scholar programs, and collaborates with universities on monetary economics, financial econometrics, and central banking history. Our engagement with the academic community strengthens research quality, attracts top talent, and ensures our analysis incorporates cutting-edge methodologies and international perspectives.

The annual Praya Economic Conference, held each September, brings together 100+ researchers from central banks, universities, and international organizations to present and discuss frontier research on monetary policy, financial stability, and economic development. Recent conference themes included "Digital Currencies and Financial Innovation," "Climate Change and Central Banking," and "Inequality and Macroeconomic Policy." Proceedings are published as a special issue in our Working Paper series.

Our Visiting Scholar Program invites 3-4 academics annually for residencies of 3-12 months. Scholars receive office space, access to proprietary data, and opportunities to collaborate with staff economists on research projects. Past visitors have contributed influential work on topics including inflation dynamics in small open economies, optimal reserve management, and the effectiveness of macro-prudential policies. The program has fostered lasting research networks benefiting both the Bank and the broader academic community.

We maintain formal research partnerships with the University of Praya's Department of Economics, the Praya School of Business, and several international institutions including the Bank for International Settlements (BIS), the International Monetary Fund (IMF), and regional central bank research networks. These collaborations facilitate staff exchanges, joint research projects, and shared conferences advancing knowledge in areas of mutual interest.

Our Research Department offers a competitive PhD internship program, hosting 5-6 doctoral students each summer to work on independent research under staff mentorship. This program supports talented students while creating a pipeline of potential future recruits familiar with central bank research priorities and institutional knowledge.

About the Bank

Independence, Transparency, Accountability

Established in 2008, the Bank of Praya operates with statutory independence to fulfill its mandate of price stability and financial system resilience.

Governance

The Bank is governed by a Board of Directors, appointed by the President and confirmed by Parliament for non-renewable seven-year terms to ensure independence from political cycles.

Governor

Dr. Elena Vasquez serves as Governor, leading the institution since 2021. She previously held senior roles at the International Monetary Fund and taught monetary economics at the University of Praya.

Mandate

The Central Bank Act (2008) grants the Bank operational independence to pursue price stability as its primary objective, with financial stability and full employment as secondary goals.

Transparency & Communication

The Governor delivers quarterly public briefings, and the Bank publishes detailed minutes of Monetary Policy Committee meetings. An annual report is submitted to Parliament each March, followed by public hearings.

Accountability

The Bank is audited annually by an external accounting firm and the National Audit Office. Financial statements, salaries of senior officials, and procurement records are published online.
